# Hook Writing

## What this skill does

This skill helps you write grammatically negative hooks that feel warm and inviting. A grammatically negative hook uses words like don't, doesn't, didn't, isn't, aren't, can't, or won't, but frames what follows as a discovery or opportunity, never a failure or correction.

## The core problem this skill solves

It's very easy to default to a small set of hook templates, especially "Most people don't know about X." That structure works, but it gets repetitive and always leans on the same angle (the reader's lack of awareness). Great hooks can negate many different things, not just knowledge.

## How to write a hook (follow these steps in order)

### Step 1. Identify the core value

Before writing anything, answer this question in plain language: what is the single biggest benefit of this tip, tool, or idea?

Write it down as a simple statement. For example:
- "Graphtreon is free and gives you great creator data"
- "This scheduling trick saves three hours a week"
- "This platform lets you find creators no one else is reaching out to"

### Step 2. Find the negation angle

Look at your core value statement and ask: what surprising or appealing thing can I negate here? The negation should come from the value itself, not from the reader's awareness of it.

There are many angles you can negate. Here are some categories to think through (these are starting points for your thinking, not templates to fill in):

- **Cost or barrier.** The thing is free, easy, or low-effort. What obstacle doesn't exist?
- **Expectation.** The thing works differently than you'd assume. What isn't true about it that you'd expect to be?
- **Exclusivity or access.** The thing is available in a way that feels surprising. What doesn't limit it?
- **Appearance vs. reality.** The thing looks unimpressive but delivers. What doesn't it look like?
- **Effort or time.** The thing takes less work than alternatives. What doesn't it require?
- **Competition or crowding.** The space isn't saturated. Who isn't doing this yet?

These categories exist to spark thinking, not to be cycled through mechanically. The goal is to find the angle that feels most true and most interesting for this specific piece of content.

### Step 3. Draft the hook

Write a short sentence using one of the grammatically negative words (don't, doesn't, didn't, isn't, aren't, can't, won't). It should:

- Be short (aim for under 12 words)
- Feel warm and inviting
- Introduce an insight or discovery
- Make the reader curious about what comes next

### Step 4. Gut-check the hook

Ask yourself these questions:

1. Does this introduce an insight or a failure? (If failure, rewrite.)
2. Could the reader feel called out or corrected? (If yes, rewrite.)
3. Have I used this same hook structure recently? (If yes, revisit Step 2 and find a different angle.)
4. Is the negation rooted in the content's actual value, or am I just negating the reader's awareness? (If the latter, revisit Step 2.)

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Graphtreon discovery tool

**Core value:** Free tool that surfaces creator data you can't find easily elsewhere.

**Angles considered:**
- Cost: It's free. "Won't cost you a thing."
- Awareness: People haven't heard of it. "Most creators don't know about this." (valid but overused)
- Effort: It does the hard work for you. "Doesn't take hours of manual research."
- Competition: Few people use it for sourcing. "Isn't on most creators' radar yet."

**Chosen hook:** "Another amazing discovery tool won't cost you a thing."

**Why this works:** The negation comes from the tool's actual value (it's free), not from the reader's ignorance. It feels generous and inviting.

### Example 2: Scheduling strategy

**Core value:** A simple calendar trick that reclaims significant time each week.

**Angles considered:**
- Effort: It's simple. "Doesn't take any extra tools."
- Time: It saves hours. "Won't eat into your creative time."
- Expectation: It seems too simple to work. "Isn't complicated at all."
- Appearance: It looks basic. "Doesn't look like much on paper."

**Chosen hook:** "This scheduling trick doesn't look like much on paper."

**Why this works:** It creates curiosity through the gap between appearance and reality. The reader wants to find out why something unimpressive actually delivers.

## The key principle

Every hook should be rooted in the content's actual value. Identify the value first, then find the most interesting way to negate something about it. Never start from a template and fill in the blanks. Start from meaning and build the sentence around it.
