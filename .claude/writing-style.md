# Vlad's Writing Style & Voice Guide

**Read this before writing any blog post, page copy, or site content.**

---

## The Core Principle

Write like you're talking to an event planner or marketing manager over coffee in NYC. Not a corporate manual. Not a pitch deck. A real conversation between someone who knows what they're doing and someone who needs it done.

---

## Voice Characteristics

- **First person when it fits:** "I've shot...", "Here's what I see...", "The companies that succeed..."
- **Opinionated but fair:** Take a position, explain why, acknowledge the other side briefly
- **Specific over vague:** "I worked with a law firm on 57th Street" beats "a typical client"
- **Direct:** Get to the point. Respects their time.
- **Problem-aware:** Show you understand their world before showing your solution

---

## Sentence & Paragraph Style

- Short sentences. Mix in medium ones.
- Paragraphs: 2–4 sentences max in body copy. 1–2 sentences on service pages.
- Flowing paragraphs that connect ideas — not isolated bullet dumps
- Each section should transition naturally into the next

---

## What to AVOID

| Don't use | Use instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| Leveraging | Using |
| Synergy | Working together |
| Best practices | What actually works |
| Game-changer | (just say what changed) |
| Seamless | Smooth / easy |
| Cutting-edge | (just describe the thing) |
| Comprehensive solutions | (be specific) |
| Bullet lists of 5+ items | Prose with 2–3 examples |
| Numbered lists (unless it's a real process) | Flowing paragraphs |
| "In today's competitive market..." | Start with a real observation |

---

## What to DO

- **Open with a specific observation or story** — not a generic intro
  - Bad: "Photography is important for businesses in today's market."
  - Good: "Here's something I've noticed shooting offices for NYC companies: the companies struggling to hire don't necessarily offer worse pay. They just look worse online."
- **Use real client stories** — anonymized if needed but specific
  - "I worked with a law firm that had 9 attorneys, all shot on different days by different photographers..."
- **Name real brands when linking** — adds credibility and SEO value
- **End each section with a transition thought** — pull the reader forward
- **CTAs should be conversational** — "Let's talk about your next event" not "Contact us today!"

---

## Blog Post Structure

1. **Hook** — specific story or counterintuitive statement (no generic intros)
2. **Body** — 5–8 H2 sections, flowing prose, max 2–3 bullet lists total per post
3. **Real examples** — name clients, describe scenarios, show don't tell
4. **CTA block** — one conversational call to action near the end

---

## Case Study Philosophy

Case studies = proof, not pitch. Show the work, answer 3 questions:
1. Can they do this? → Photos/video
2. What did it cost? → Transparent pricing
3. How do I ask? → Simple CTA

Cut: educational content, ROI explanations, selling copy, service descriptions.
Keep: the work itself, brief context (2 sentences), results as numbers, clear next step.
Target length: ~400 words max.

---

## Page Copy Principles

**Pain points Core Visuals solves (weave these in naturally):**
- Agency bloat → "Direct access to the person actually shooting your event"
- Hidden costs → "Fixed packages. No surprise fees."
- Slow turnaround → "Content delivered while your event is still relevant"
- Inconsistent quality → "Same team every time"

**Positioning language:**
- "Boutique by design" (not "boutique" alone — explain what it means concretely)
- "Production company" (not just "videographer" — better SEO + positioning)
- "Transparent pricing" (say it, don't just imply it)

---

## What "Boutique" Actually Means (Use These Concrete Explanations)

Don't just say boutique. Say what it means:
- No account managers between you and the person with the camera
- Fixed packages, no mystery pricing
- Small team = lower overhead = better rates than agencies
- Same person from first email to final delivery
